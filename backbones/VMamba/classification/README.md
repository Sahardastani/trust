### Training and Finetuning details

We trained VMamba with batch normalization layer on ImageNet-1K dataset using a learning rate of 5e-4 for 150 epochs. To enable evaluation on corrupted datasets with different label spaces than ImageNet-1k, we fine-tune only the classifier layer on the clean version of each target dataset while keeping the rest of the model frozen. This procedure is applied to **CIFAR10-C**, **CIFAR100-C**, and **PACS**. For PACS, which includes four domains (photo, art painting, cartoon, and sketch), we follow the standard protocol by holding out one domain for evaluation and training on the remaining three. Specifically, we use the photo domain as the held-out test set. For datasets such as **ImageNet-Sketch**, **ImageNet-V2**, and **ImageNet-R**, which share the same label space as ImageNet, no fine-tuning is required.

We also explored the layer-normalization-based VMamba variant in our preliminary experiments. In our setting, this variant showed unstable behavior on some ImageNet-C corruption types, even under a simple adaptation baseline that updates only the Mamba-specific parameters and normalization layers, without any additional modification. For several corruptions, such as glass blur and fog, the accuracy became unusually low and in some cases new zero. These observations indicate that the normalization choice plays an important role for test-time adaptation under distribution shift in VMamba. Because VMamba updates a recurrent hidden state, errors introduced at one step can affect many later steps (Equation 8). When we adapt Mamba-specific parameters, we are directly changing the dynamics that control these hidden-state updates. In this setting, the model becomes quite sensitive to the scale and distribution of intermediate activations. In our experiments, under the same setting, the batch-normalization-based variant was more stable. Therefore, we use the batch-normalization-based VMamba variant as the common baseline for all methods compared in our paper.
